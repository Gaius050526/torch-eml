\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The experiments presented in Section~\ref{sec:experiments} demonstrate that the
\textsc{ComposeHead} architecture can recover exact closed-form solutions to the
two-dimensional Navier--Stokes equations from data.  In this section we examine
the scope of this capability, its relationship to existing work, and the
limitations that constrain its present applicability.

\paragraph{What the method can do.}
The method discovers closed-form PDE solutions whenever the true solution lies
within the span of the EML primitive basis---that is, when it can be expressed
as a linear combination of products of $\sin$, $\cos$, $\exp$, $\ln$, and the
identity function applied along individual coordinate axes.  This class
encompasses a substantial fraction of the analytical solutions catalogued in
classical references: separable solutions to the heat, wave, and
advection--diffusion equations; Fourier modes of elliptic boundary-value
problems; and exponentially decaying vortex solutions of the Navier--Stokes
equations, among others.  The method is particularly effective for separable
solutions, where the product-of-univariates structure of the
ansatz~\eqref{eq:composehead} aligns exactly with the form of the target
expression.

\paragraph{What the method cannot do.}
Equally important are the boundaries of the approach.  The \textsc{ComposeHead}
does not prove existence or regularity of solutions in the PDE-theoretic
sense.  It does not address uniqueness, nor does it provide any guarantee that
a discovered solution is the physically relevant one among potentially many
solutions to the same equations.  Turbulent flows, whose velocity fields lack
any known closed-form representation, lie entirely outside the method's reach.
More generally, any solution that cannot be expressed as a finite linear
combination of separable products of the available primitives is
undiscoverable: the architecture can only find what its basis can represent.
Finally, the method is data-driven and requires training data---obtained from
simulation, experiment, or collocation-point sampling---to guide the
optimization.

\paragraph{Relationship to the Navier--Stokes Millennium Prize.}
The recovery of the Taylor--Green vortex solution naturally raises the question
of how this work relates to the Clay Mathematics Institute Millennium Prize
Problem concerning the Navier--Stokes equations.  We wish to be precise on this
point.  The Millennium Prize requires proving, for the three-dimensional
incompressible Navier--Stokes equations, that smooth solutions exist globally in
time for arbitrary smooth initial data of finite energy, or exhibiting a
counterexample.  Our work addresses a fundamentally different question: given
specific initial conditions, can one algorithmically discover an explicit
closed-form expression that satisfies the equations?  The result is a
\emph{constructive existence demonstration} for a particular initial datum---we
exhibit the solution rather than proving that one must exist.  This is distinct
from, and considerably weaker than, the general existence and smoothness claim
demanded by the Millennium Prize.  Nonetheless, the ability to algorithmically
discover closed-form solutions for families of initial conditions may inform
theoretical investigations by providing a growing library of exact solutions
whose analytic properties can be studied directly.

\paragraph{Comparison to symbolic regression.}
PySR~\cite{cranmer2023} and AI~Feynman~\cite{udrescu2020} search over arbitrary
expression trees using genetic programming and physics-inspired decomposition
strategies, respectively.  These methods are strictly more general than ours:
they can discover algebraic relationships of essentially any form, subject to
computational budget.  Our approach trades this generality for structure.  By
constraining the search to a physically motivated basis of axis-aligned
separable terms, we exploit the observation that PDE solutions are
overwhelmingly products of elementary functions of individual variables.  This
narrower search space yields two practical advantages.  First, the optimization
landscape is better conditioned: the number of free parameters scales linearly
in the number of terms rather than exponentially in expression depth.  Second,
the separable structure makes the discovery of multi-variable solutions
tractable, whereas generic symbolic regression must search over an
exponentially larger space of nested compositions.  The trade-off is that
arbitrary algebraic relationships---such as $E = mc^{2}$ or other non-separable
laws---fall outside the ansatz.

\paragraph{The role of normalization.}
The parameterization $c_k \cdot f(\alpha_{k,d}\, x_d + \beta_{k,d})$ admits
gauge freedom: multiple parameter settings can encode the same function.  For
example, $\sin(x - \pi) \cdot \exp(b) \cdot c = \sin(x) \cdot c'$ where
$c' = -\exp(b) \cdot c$, because $\sin(x - \pi) = -\sin(x)$.  Similarly, a
cosine with a phase offset of $\pi/2$ is a sine, and exponential biases can be
absorbed into multiplicative coefficients.  Without normalization, gradient-based
optimization converges to one of many equivalent parameter settings, and
coefficient snapping---which relies on the learned parameters being close to
canonical values---fails.  The normalization pipeline described in
Section~\ref{sec:method} resolves this gauge freedom by absorbing exponential
biases into the linear coefficients~$c_k$, canonicalizing trigonometric phases
using the identities $\sin(x + \pi/2) = \cos(x)$ and $\cos(x + \pi) =
-\cos(x)$, and reducing all phase offsets to the interval $[0, \pi/2)$.  This
canonicalization is essential: it is what transforms a continuously
parameterized model into an expression that coefficient snapping can convert to
exact symbolic form.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
We summarize the principal limitations of the current method.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Data dependence.}  The method requires training data, whether from
    numerical simulation, physical experiment, or dense collocation-point
    sampling.  It cannot discover solutions from the PDE alone without some form
    of supervision.

  \item \emph{Separability assumption.}  The axis-aligned separable
    ansatz~\eqref{eq:composehead} cannot represent non-separable solutions.
    Self-similar solutions of the form $u(x,t) = t^{-\alpha} g(x/t^{\beta})$,
    traveling-wave solutions $u(x,t) = f(x - ct)$, and other structures in which
    the variables are coupled through a shared argument lie outside the current
    hypothesis class.

  \item \emph{Combinatorial scaling.}  The number of candidate terms scales as
    $|\mathcal{F}|^{K} \times D^{K}$ for $K$-factor products drawn from a
    primitive library~$\mathcal{F}$ over $D$ coordinate dimensions.  While this
    is far smaller than the space of arbitrary expression trees, it remains
    exponential in $K$ and may become prohibitive for high-dimensional problems
    or large primitive libraries.

  \item \emph{Dimensionality.}  The experiments presented here are restricted to
    two spatial dimensions plus time.  Extension to three-dimensional
    problems is conceptually straightforward but has not yet been validated.

  \item \emph{Basis completeness.}  The primitive library must contain the
    functions appearing in the true solution.  If the solution involves a
    function not in the basis---for instance, a Bessel function or an elliptic
    integral---the method cannot discover it, regardless of the quality or
    quantity of training data.
\end{itemize}


\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future}

Several directions extend the framework presented in this paper.

\paragraph{Three-dimensional Navier--Stokes solutions.}
The most immediate extension is to three spatial dimensions.  A number of exact
solutions to the 3D incompressible Navier--Stokes equations are known, including
Beltrami flows and the Arnold--Beltrami--Childress (ABC) flow, whose velocity
fields are separable products of trigonometric functions in $x$, $y$, and $z$.
These solutions lie squarely within the hypothesis class of the
\textsc{ComposeHead} and would provide a direct test of the method's scalability
to higher-dimensional domains.

\paragraph{Physics-informed discovery without training data.}
In the present work, the \textsc{ComposeHead} is trained on data generated by a
known analytical solution.  A natural next step is to eliminate the data
requirement entirely and train using only the PDE residual loss, boundary
conditions, and initial conditions---in the spirit of physics-informed neural
networks but with a symbolic rather than neural ansatz.  This would transform
the method from a symbolic regression tool into a genuine symbolic PDE solver.

\paragraph{Non-separable and nested compositions.}
The separability assumption is the most restrictive aspect of the current
framework.  Two strategies may extend the method to non-separable solutions:
(i)~introducing mixed terms of the form $f(a x + b y)$, which allow coupling
between coordinate variables, and (ii)~permitting nested compositions such as
$f \circ g$ within each axis-aligned factor.  Both extensions enlarge the
hypothesis class at the cost of increased combinatorial complexity, and
principled strategies for managing this trade-off remain to be developed.

\paragraph{Automated basis selection.}
Currently, the primitive library is specified manually.  An adaptive approach
that begins with a small library and progressively introduces additional
primitives based on residual analysis could reduce the combinatorial burden for
problems where the relevant function class is unknown a priori.

\paragraph{Scaling to higher-dimensional PDEs.}
PDEs arising in kinetic theory, finance, and quantum mechanics may involve five
or more independent variables.  The exponential scaling of the candidate term
count with dimensionality demands algorithmic innovations---sparsity-promoting
regularization, hierarchical decomposition, or greedy term selection---to remain
tractable.

\paragraph{Integration with existing PDE solver libraries.}
The \textsc{ComposeHead} could serve as a post-processing module for existing
numerical solvers, accepting high-fidelity simulation data as input and
returning symbolic expressions as output.  Hybrid workflows that alternate
between numerical solution and symbolic identification may prove especially
effective for parameter studies, where an analytical expression discovered at one
parameter value can seed the search at nearby values.


\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have presented the \textsc{ComposeHead}, an architecture for discovering
closed-form solutions to partial differential equations using verified primitive
functions constructed from the EML universal basis.  The key architectural
innovation---axis-aligned separable terms composed of trainable affine
transformations of elementary functions---makes product-of-univariates solutions
directly discoverable by gradient-based optimization, reducing the symbolic
regression problem from an exponentially large search over expression trees to a
structured optimization over a small number of scalar parameters.

We demonstrated the method on the two-dimensional incompressible Navier--Stokes
equations, where it recovered the Taylor--Green vortex solution
$u = \sin(x)\cos(y)\exp(-t/5)$,\;$v = -\cos(x)\sin(y)\exp(-t/5)$
at machine precision, achieving pointwise errors below $10^{-6}$ after
normalization and coefficient snapping.  The resulting expressions are not
neural-network approximations but genuine symbolic formulas: they can be
analytically differentiated, substituted into the governing equations, and
verified to satisfy the Navier--Stokes system exactly.

We release \texttt{torch-eml} as an open-source PyTorch library at
\url{https://github.com/gaius050526/torch-eml}, providing verified EML
primitive implementations, the \textsc{ComposeHead} architecture, and the
normalization and snapping pipeline described in this work.

While the current method is limited to separable solutions in two spatial
dimensions and requires the primitive basis to contain the relevant functions,
the framework establishes a foundation for symbolic PDE solving that occupies a
distinctive position between neural solvers and classical analytical methods.
Neural solvers offer generality at the cost of interpretability; analytical
methods offer exactness but require human ingenuity.  The approach presented
here automates the discovery of exact, interpretable solutions within a
structured function class, bridging the gap between these two paradigms.
